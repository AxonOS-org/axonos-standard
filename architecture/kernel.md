# Architecture: The Real-Time Microkernel

*An architecture chapter of the AxonOS Standard. Informative throughout. This chapter elaborates the design and implementation of the kernel; the normative requirements it must satisfy are in Part II of `STANDARD.md`. Where this chapter and the Standard differ, the Standard governs.*

---

## 1. Purpose and scope of this chapter

The AxonOS kernel is the component on which the entire timing argument of the Standard rests. It is the deterministic substrate: the piece of software that ingests a stream of digital samples from the acquisition layer, runs a fixed signal-processing pipeline against them under a provable timing bound, and emits a stream of typed intent observations to the application layer. Everything else in an AxonOS deployment — the software development kit, the application, the consent subsystem's user-facing parts — depends on the kernel having done its job within its time budget, and the credibility of every latency number the project publishes traces back to the kernel's worst-case response time.

This chapter explains how the kernel is built and, more importantly, why it is built that way. It is a long chapter because the kernel embodies a large number of individually small decisions, and each of those decisions trades something away — average-case speed, flexibility, the convenience of a feature — in exchange for worst-case provability. A reader who sees only the individual decisions, without the unifying objective, will find many of them perverse. A reader who holds the objective firmly in mind — *a provable, small worst case, on commodity hardware, with structural memory safety* — will find that each decision falls out of the objective almost inevitably.

The chapter proceeds from the objective, through the three structural commitments that the objective forces, through the concrete architecture of the dual-core system, the task set, the scheduler, the inter-process communication channel, and the clock, to a frank discussion of what makes the kernel genuinely difficult to get right and where its boundaries lie. It closes with the kernel's relationship to the other subsystems and a summary of its measured behaviour on the reference hardware.

## 2. The single design objective

It is worth stating the kernel's design objective in one sentence and then dwelling on every word of it, because the sentence is load-bearing and the words are chosen precisely.

*The kernel provides a provable, small worst-case response time, on commodity hardware, with structural memory safety.*

**Provable.** Not measured, not benchmarked, not typical — provable. The kernel's central claim is an upper bound on how long the signal-processing pipeline can take, and an upper bound is a statement about every possible execution, including executions that have never been observed. A claim of that form cannot be established by running the pipeline a million times and recording the slowest run, because the slowest run out of a million is still only the slowest of those million, and the one that would have been slower lay in the unsampled remainder of the input space. A genuine upper bound is established by an argument — a proof — that ranges over the entire input space at once. The kernel is architected, throughout, to keep that proof tractable: every design decision that would make the proof harder, or impossible, is rejected in favour of one that keeps the proof within reach of a bounded model checker.

**Small.** The worst-case response time must not merely be bounded; it must be bounded by a number small enough to fit, with comfortable margin, inside a processing epoch. In the reference configuration the epoch is four milliseconds and the proven bound is one millisecond, a factor of four of margin. The smallness matters because the user experiences the worst case, not the average: a pipeline that is usually fast but occasionally slow feels, to the user, like an unreliable interface, and no amount of good average-case behaviour repairs that feeling. Smallness of the worst case is smallness of the thing the user actually feels.

**On commodity hardware.** The kernel runs on parts that anyone can buy from ordinary distributors: a Cortex-M4F microcontroller, a widely used analog front end, a common Bluetooth radio. It does not require a custom application-specific integrated circuit, a field-programmable gate array, or any exotic or single-sourced component. This is a deliberate constraint and it has a cost — a custom chip could do some of this work faster — but the constraint buys two things the project values more than raw speed. It buys reproducibility: an independent team can buy the same parts and re-run the project's measurements, which is what makes the project's evidence-level-L2 claims meaningful. And it buys longevity: a design that depends only on commodity parts is not hostage to a single vendor's decision to discontinue a specialised component.

**Structural memory safety.** The kernel is written so that whole classes of bug — buffer overruns, use-after-free errors, data races, the reading of uninitialised memory — are not caught at runtime but are prevented at compile time, by the type system of the implementation language. The kernel forbids the `unsafe` escape hatch of its implementation language entirely; it compiles to a binary in which there is no unchecked pointer arithmetic, no unsynchronised shared mutable state, no manual memory management. Memory safety in the kernel is not a property the developers are careful to maintain; it is a property the compiler refuses to let them violate.

Every section that follows is, in one way or another, this objective worked out in detail.

## 3. The three structural commitments

The objective of Section 2 forces three large structural commitments. They are large in the sense that they are not local implementation choices but pervasive properties of the whole kernel, and they are commitments in the sense that, once made, they constrain every subsequent decision. The three are: the implementation language is Rust, used in its `no_std` configuration with the unsafe escape hatch forbidden; the scheduling discipline is Earliest-Deadline-First; and the inter-core communication is a lock-free single-producer single-consumer channel. This section takes each in turn.

### 3.1 Rust, `no_std`, `forbid(unsafe_code)`

The kernel is written in Rust. More precisely, it is written in Rust with the `#![no_std]` attribute applied at the crate root, which severs the kernel from the Rust standard library and leaves it with only the `core` library — the subset of Rust's facilities that does not assume an operating system, a heap, or a filesystem beneath it — and it is written with the `#![forbid(unsafe_code)]` attribute applied at the crate root, which makes it a compile-time error for any code anywhere in the kernel to use the `unsafe` keyword.

The choice of Rust over the obvious alternative, C, was made deliberately and was made the hard way: the project's first kernel prototype was written in C, and was abandoned. C is the traditional language of embedded real-time systems, the language of the largest body of existing real-time code, the language with the most mature embedded toolchains, and the project's initial instinct was to use it. That instinct survived contact with three months of development and a sequence of memory-corruption bugs — an off-by-one in a ring-buffer index, a pointer that outlived the buffer it pointed into, a structure field read before it was written — each of which was individually small, individually a few minutes to fix once found, and individually capable, in a safety-critical neural-interface kernel, of causing a silent and dangerous malfunction. What the sequence of bugs taught the project was not that its developers were careless but that C's safety model — careful review, discipline, convention — does not scale to a safety-critical system maintained, as this one largely is, by a small team. C asks every developer, on every line, to be perfect, and offers no mechanism to catch the inevitable lapses; the lapses are caught, if at all, by testing, and testing samples the execution space exactly the way measurement samples the input space, which is to say incompletely.

Rust's safety model is different in kind. The classes of bug that the C prototype kept producing — the buffer overruns, the use-after-free errors, the data races — are, in safe Rust, not bugs that discipline must prevent but constructions that the compiler refuses to accept. The borrow checker, Rust's compile-time analysis of which references to which data are valid for which spans of the program, rejects, before the program ever runs, the use of a reference after the data it refers to has been freed; it rejects the existence of two mutable references to the same data at the same time, which is the structural precondition of a data race; it rejects indexing past the end of an array, by inserting a bounds check that the type system guarantees is present. None of this is discipline. It is the compiler's refusal, and the compiler does not have lapses.

The `#![no_std]` configuration is forced by the deployment target. The kernel runs on a microcontroller with a few hundred kilobytes of static memory and no operating system; the Rust standard library assumes an operating system beneath it, assumes a heap allocator, assumes a filesystem, and none of those assumptions holds. The `core` library, which `#![no_std]` leaves available, assumes none of them, and it is sufficient: it provides the language's fundamental types, its arithmetic, its slices and iterators and option and result types, everything the kernel's logic needs, and it provides them with the same compile-time safety guarantees as the full standard library.

The `#![forbid(unsafe_code)]` attribute is the strongest of the three and deserves the most comment. Rust's safety guarantees are not absolute by default; the language provides the `unsafe` keyword, which marks a region of code in which certain compiler checks are suspended and the developer takes manual responsibility for upholding the invariants the checks would have enforced. `unsafe` exists for good reasons — it is how Rust code talks to hardware registers, how it implements the lowest-level data structures, how the standard library itself is built — and a great deal of correct, safe-in-practice Rust uses `unsafe` carefully. But `unsafe` is also, precisely, the place where the memory-safety bugs of the C prototype could creep back in, because inside an `unsafe` block the compiler's refusal is suspended and the developer is once again asked to be perfect. The kernel forbids `unsafe` entirely. The cost of this is real: the kernel cannot, itself, directly poke a hardware register, and must instead reach hardware through a thin, separately audited hardware-abstraction layer that is permitted `unsafe` and that the kernel treats as a trusted dependency. The benefit is that the kernel proper — the scheduler, the pipeline orchestration, the inter-process communication logic, the consent integration, all the code that constitutes the kernel's own logic — contains not one line in which the compiler's memory-safety refusal is suspended. The kernel's memory safety is, for the kernel's own code, total and compiler-enforced.

### 3.2 Earliest-Deadline-First scheduling

The kernel schedules the tasks of its signal-processing core under the Earliest-Deadline-First discipline. The Standard requires this, in Section 7.1, and the Standard's Section 7.2 gives the theoretical justification; this subsection adds the implementation perspective.

Under Earliest-Deadline-First the scheduler, at every scheduling decision, runs the ready task whose absolute deadline is nearest. The deadline is computed, for each task, as the task's release time plus its relative deadline; the release time advances each period; and so a task's effective priority — its position in the queue of ready tasks — is not a fixed number assigned once but a quantity recomputed continuously as deadlines approach and recede. This is what makes Earliest-Deadline-First a dynamic-priority discipline, in contrast to the fixed-priority Rate-Monotonic discipline in which each task's priority is assigned at design time and never changes.

From the implementation's point of view, Earliest-Deadline-First is slightly more work per scheduling decision than a fixed-priority discipline, because the scheduler must compare deadlines rather than simply consult a static priority, and the kernel's scheduler implementation accounts for that comparison in its own worst-case execution time. But the cost is small and bounded — the task set is small and fixed, so the comparison is over a small fixed set — and it buys the two properties the Standard's Section 7.2 describes: the clean utilisation bound of exactly 1.0, and the stability of the analysis under revision of the task set. The second of these is, for the kernel's maintainers, the more valuable day to day. The signal-processing pipeline is not frozen; it will be revised, across the lifetime of the Standard, as filtering improves and as new intent categories are added, and every revision changes the task set. Under Earliest-Deadline-First, re-validating the timing after a task-set revision is the recomputation of a single utilisation sum and a single re-run of the response-time analysis; under Rate-Monotonic it would be the re-derivation of a priority assignment and the re-checking of every task against every higher-priority task. The kernel's maintainers will perform that re-validation many times, and the discipline whose re-validation is cheap and mechanical is the discipline that will actually be re-validated correctly each time.

### 3.3 The lock-free single-producer single-consumer channel

The kernel communicates with the application core through a lock-free, single-producer, single-consumer ring buffer in shared memory. The Standard requires this in Section 10; this subsection explains the implementation reasoning.

The word that does the work in "lock-free" is *lock*. A lock — a mutex, a semaphore, any construct by which one party waits for another to relinquish access to shared data — introduces, by its nature, the possibility that the party holding the lock delays the party waiting for it. In a channel between the deterministic signal-processing core and the non-deterministic application core, a lock would mean that the signal-processing core's publication task could be made to wait for the application core, and the application core's timing is, by construction, not bounded: the general-purpose operating system on the application core can preempt the software development kit at any moment, for any duration. A lock on the channel would therefore import the application core's non-determinism into the signal-processing core's timing analysis, and the timing analysis would collapse. The channel must be lock-free precisely so that the publication task's worst-case execution time depends only on the publication task, never on the consumer.

The words "single-producer, single-consumer" describe the simplest possible concurrency pattern: exactly one writer, exactly one reader. This simplicity is itself a design choice in service of the timing proof. A multi-producer or multi-consumer channel requires, in general, atomic compare-and-swap operations whose worst-case timing under contention is subtle to bound; a single-producer single-consumer channel can be implemented with only plain reads, plain writes, and memory barriers, every one of which has a simple and tight worst-case time. The kernel has exactly one task that writes the channel and the software development kit is exactly one component that reads it, so the single-producer single-consumer pattern is not a limitation the kernel works around but a precise match to the kernel's actual structure, and the kernel takes the timing-analysis simplicity that the match makes available.

## 4. The dual-core architecture in detail

The kernel's hardware substrate is a dual-core system, and the two cores have sharply different characters. This section describes each core, the bridge between them, and the discipline that keeps the non-determinism of one core from contaminating the other.

### 4.1 The signal-processing core

The signal-processing core is, in the reference hardware, an ARM Cortex-M4F running at 168 megahertz. Three properties of this core recommend it for the role.

First, its instruction timing is, to a degree unusual among modern processors, deterministic and documented. The Cortex-M4F has a simple in-order pipeline, no speculative execution of the kind that makes timing on larger processors data-dependent in hard-to-bound ways, and a documented cycle count for each instruction. This determinism is exactly what the worst-case-execution-time analysis of the pipeline tasks needs: an analysis that must bound the time of a sequence of instructions can do so tightly only if each instruction's time is itself tightly known.

Second, it has a hardware floating-point unit — the F in M4F. The signal-processing pipeline is arithmetic-heavy: the Kalman filter, the finite-impulse-response and notch filters, the spatial-pattern feature extraction all multiply and add floating-point numbers in quantity. A core without a hardware floating-point unit would emulate that arithmetic in software, slowly and with a worst-case time much harder to bound. The hardware unit makes the arithmetic fast and makes its timing simple.

Third, the development tooling for the Cortex-M family is mature. Debuggers, on-chip-debugging probes, the compiler back-end, the bounded model checker's support for the target — all of it exists, is maintained, and is widely used. A kernel is hard enough to get right without also fighting an immature toolchain, and the maturity of the Cortex-M tooling removes that fight.

The signal-processing core runs nothing but the kernel. It does not run an operating system; it does not run application code; it runs the kernel's scheduler and the kernel's task set and nothing else. This exclusivity is the foundation of the timing analysis: the analysis must account for everything that can consume the signal-processing core's cycles, and it can do so completely only because the set of things that consume those cycles is the kernel's own fixed task set plus a known set of interrupts, with nothing else admitted.

### 4.2 The application core

The application core is, in the reference hardware, an ARM Cortex-A53 running at 1.2 gigahertz or above, executing a general-purpose operating system, typically a Linux derivative. The application core's character is the opposite of the signal-processing core's: it is fast, it is flexible, it runs a rich software environment — and it is not deterministic. The general-purpose operating system on the application core can, at any moment and for any duration, preempt the software development kit to run a kernel thread of its own, to service an interrupt, to reclaim memory, to migrate a process between its own cores. The application core's timing is, from the point of view of a hard-real-time analysis, simply unknown.

This is acceptable, and indeed it is the right division of labour, because the application core does not bear a real-time obligation. Its job is to run the application and the software development kit, and the application's job — moving a cursor, updating a display, recording an event — is soft-real-time at worst: a few milliseconds of jitter in the application's response is imperceptible or nearly so, where the same jitter in the signal-processing core's pipeline would be a failure. The architecture puts the deterministic work on the deterministic core and the flexible work on the flexible core, and the only thing it must be careful about is the boundary between them.

### 4.3 The bridge and the containment of non-determinism

The two cores share a region of static random-access memory, reachable from both across a deterministic bus bridge. The shared region holds the inter-process-communication ring buffer of Section 6, and the ring buffer is the *only* coupling between the two cores. There is no other shared mutable state; there is no mechanism by which the application core can call into the signal-processing core, inject a task, alter a schedule, or touch a kernel data structure. The coupling is exactly one ring buffer, written by the signal-processing core and read by the application core, and the discipline of Section 6 — lock-free, single-producer single-consumer, with the sequence-number consistency check — is what ensures that this single coupling transmits *data* from the deterministic core to the non-deterministic core without transmitting *non-determinism* in the other direction. The signal-processing core writes the ring buffer and never waits on it; therefore nothing the application core does, no matter how slow or how preempted the application core becomes, can delay the signal-processing core. The non-determinism is contained on the application core's side of the bridge, and the timing analysis of the signal-processing core is sound because the containment holds.

## 5. The task set and the pipeline

The signal-processing core's work, within each four-millisecond epoch, is the execution of a fixed pipeline of signal-processing stages, each realised as a scheduler task. This section describes the pipeline and the discipline that keeps the task set fixed.

### 5.1 The pipeline stages

The reference pipeline has six signal-processing stages, executed in sequence within the epoch, plus a seventh task that publishes the result.

The pipeline begins with a **Kalman-filter state update**, which maintains a running estimate of the underlying neural state and uses it to smooth the incoming samples, reducing the influence of moment-to-moment measurement noise. The Kalman filter is a predictor-corrector: it predicts the current state from the previous one, compares the prediction to the new measurement, and corrects. Its worst-case execution time on the reference hardware is eighty microseconds.

The smoothed stream then passes through a **finite-impulse-response filter**, which shapes the signal's frequency content, attenuating frequencies outside the band where the intent-relevant activity lies. The finite-impulse-response filter is the pipeline's most expensive stage, at three hundred and twenty microseconds, because it convolves each of the eight channels against a substantial filter kernel.

A **notch filter** follows, removing the narrow-band interference at the local electrical-mains frequency — fifty or sixty hertz depending on the region — that contaminates almost every biosignal recording. The notch filter is cheap, at forty microseconds, because it targets a single narrow frequency.

An **artefact-rejection stage**, fifteen microseconds, identifies epochs in which a muscle movement, an electrode disturbance, or another non-neural event has corrupted the signal, and flags them so that the downstream stages and the application can discount them.

A **common-spatial-pattern feature extractor**, one hundred and sixty microseconds, transforms the multi-channel signal into a small set of features chosen to maximise the discriminability of the intent categories — it finds the spatial combinations of channels that best separate, say, an intended leftward movement from an intended rightward one.

A **linear-discriminant classifier**, twenty-five microseconds, takes those features and produces the actual decision: which intent, if any, the epoch contains, and with what confidence.

Finally, the **inter-process-communication publication task**, two-tenths of a microsecond, writes the classifier's conclusion as an intent observation into the ring buffer.

The sum of these worst-case execution times is approximately six hundred and forty microseconds, and against the four-millisecond epoch that is a utilisation of about 0.160 — well within the 0.25 ceiling, with the remaining budget reserved for interrupt servicing and for the conservative margins of the analysis.

### 5.2 Why the task set must be fixed

The Standard's Section 6.3 requires the task set to be fixed and known at the time the timing analysis is performed, and forbids runtime addition of tasks or runtime change of a task's timing parameters except through the admission-control procedure. The kernel honours this absolutely, and it is worth being concrete about why.

The worst-case response time is proven for a specific task set. The proof's input is the set of tasks, each with its worst-case execution time and its period; the proof's output is the bound. Change the input — add a task, lengthen a task, shorten a period — and the old proof's output is no longer about the system that is now running. It is not that the old bound becomes merely approximate; it becomes a bound for a different system, and for the system actually running there is, until the proof is re-done, no bound at all. A kernel that allowed the task set to change without re-proving would be a kernel that, in the interval between the change and a re-proof that might never happen, was running with no valid timing guarantee while still presenting itself as a hard-real-time system. The fixed-task-set discipline, and the admission-control gate that is the sole exception to it, exist so that the kernel is never in that state: the task set is what the proof covers, and any change to it passes through admission control, which re-validates before permitting the change.

## 6. The scheduler implementation

This section describes how the Earliest-Deadline-First discipline is realised in the kernel's code, and how the scheduler itself is kept analysable.

### 6.1 The scheduling decision

The scheduler is invoked at each scheduler tick and at each event that can change the set of ready tasks — the release of a task at its period boundary, the completion of a task. At each invocation it performs a single conceptual operation: among the ready tasks, find the one with the nearest absolute deadline, and run it, preempting whatever was running if the newly chosen task differs from it.

Because the task set is small and fixed, "find the one with the nearest absolute deadline" is a search over a small fixed collection, and its worst-case time is therefore a small fixed quantity that the scheduler's own worst-case-execution-time analysis includes. The scheduler does not use a dynamic data structure whose size or shape depends on runtime history; it uses a fixed array indexed by a fixed set of task identifiers, and every operation on that array is a bounded loop over a known length. This is a deliberate choice: a more sophisticated scheduler might use a priority heap or a balanced tree to make the scheduling decision asymptotically faster, but asymptotic speed is irrelevant for a fixed small task set, and the dynamic data structure would make the scheduler's own worst-case time harder to bound. The kernel chooses the simple fixed-array scheduler precisely because its timing is trivial to analyse.

### 6.2 Deadline-miss detection

Clause DC4 of the dual-core contract requires that a missed deadline be detected within two scheduler ticks. The scheduler implements this by checking, at each tick, whether any task that should have completed by now has not, and raising a deadline-miss condition if so. The check is a bounded loop over the fixed task set, comparing each task's completion status against its deadline, and its timing is part of the scheduler's analysed worst-case time. A detected deadline miss is not, in a correctly admitted task set, supposed to be reachable — the whole timing analysis exists to prove it cannot happen — and so the deadline-miss path is, in effect, a last-line-of-defence assertion: if the analysis was somehow wrong, or if a hardware fault perturbed the timing, the kernel detects the miss promptly and can react, rather than continuing silently with a violated guarantee.

## 7. The inter-process-communication channel implementation

The Standard's Section 10 specifies the inter-process-communication channel; this section describes how the kernel implements it and, in particular, how it implements the sequence-number consistency check that makes the lock-free channel safe.

### 7.1 The ring buffer layout

The channel is a ring buffer of fixed-size slots in the shared memory region. Each slot is thirty-two bytes: the twenty-eight-byte intent-observation record followed by a four-byte trailing copy of the low thirty-two bits of the record's sequence number. The slot count is a fixed power of two, so that the modular arithmetic of advancing a ring index is a simple bitwise mask rather than a division. The buffer has a write index, owned by the producer, and a read index, owned by the consumer; the producer never writes the read index and the consumer never writes the write index, which is what makes the channel genuinely single-producer single-consumer at the level of every individual memory location.

### 7.2 The publication sequence

When the publication task has an intent observation to publish, it performs a fixed sequence of operations. It writes the observation's sequence number into the leading bytes of the slot. It writes the remaining twenty-four bytes of the observation record. It issues a memory barrier, which ensures that all the writes so far become visible to the other core before any subsequent write does. It writes the four-byte trailing copy of the sequence number. It issues a second barrier. It advances the write index.

The order is the entire point. The trailing sequence-number copy is written *after* the body and *after* a barrier, so the trailing copy becomes visible to the consumer only once the body is already visible. Therefore, if the consumer sees a particular value in the trailing copy, the consumer is guaranteed that the body associated with that value is already fully written and visible.

### 7.3 The consumption sequence and the consistency check

When the consumer wishes to read a slot, it reads the four-byte trailing sequence-number copy first. It then reads the body of the slot. It then reads the leading sequence number from within the record. If the leading and trailing sequence numbers are equal, the consumer concludes that the slot was written completely — leading number, body, trailing number all from the same publication — before the consumer began reading, and the slot is valid. If they differ, the consumer concludes that the producer was writing the slot concurrently with the consumer's read: the producer had advanced the leading number for a new publication but the consumer caught the slot mid-write, and the body the consumer read is torn, a mixture of the old publication and the new. In that case the consumer discards the read and retries.

This is the classic seqlock pattern, and its correctness rests on the memory barriers of the publication sequence. The barriers guarantee that the visibility order matches the write order, and the write order — leading number, body, trailing number — is what makes the equality of the leading and trailing numbers a reliable witness that the body between them is consistent. The pattern is lock-free in the strict sense: the producer never waits, the consumer never waits, the consumer's only response to a detected conflict is to retry, and a retry is bounded because the producer makes progress on every attempt. The kernel's worst-case analysis of the publication task includes the fixed cost of the write sequence and the barriers, and that fixed cost — two-tenths of a microsecond on the reference hardware — is the DC3 figure.

## 8. The clock

The kernel exposes a single monotonic clock, derived from the cycle-counter register of the signal-processing core. The cycle counter increments once per processor clock cycle and is never written by the kernel, only read; the kernel converts cycle counts to microseconds by a fixed division by the known clock rate. Because the counter is hardware, free-running, and never adjusted, the clock the kernel derives from it is monotonic in the strict sense the Standard requires: two reads of the clock can return equal values, if they fall within the same microsecond, but they can never return decreasing values. The kernel represents the clock as a sixty-four-bit microsecond count, which, as the Standard notes, does not wrap for a span of time vastly longer than any device's service life, so the kernel contains no wrap-handling logic and is free of the bugs such logic tends to harbour.

## 9. What makes the kernel genuinely difficult

It would be misleading to present the kernel as a straightforward application of well-understood principles. It is difficult, and honesty about where the difficulty lies is more useful to an implementer than a false impression of ease. The difficulty concentrates in three places.

The first is that the pipeline is a **critical path with no parallelism to exploit**. The six signal-processing stages must execute in sequence — each consumes the previous one's output — and they execute on a single core, so there is no opportunity to overlap them. The only way to reduce the pipeline's worst-case response time is to reduce the worst-case execution time of the individual stages, and each stage has already been optimised. The kernel's worst-case response time of nine hundred and seventy-two microseconds, against the one-millisecond bound, was not reached easily; it was reached by successive rounds of optimisation of the most expensive stages, and the initial, unoptimised pipeline exceeded the bound. An implementer should expect the timing to be tight and the optimisation to be real work.

The second is that the **interrupt landscape is hostile to the analysis**. The signal-processing core, although it runs only the kernel's task set, is interrupted by the analog-to-digital converter signalling that a sample is ready, by the inter-core bridge signalling activity, by the radio. Each interrupt steals cycles from whatever task is running, and the timing analysis must account for the worst-case interference each interrupt source can inflict. Bounding that interference requires knowing the maximum rate of each interrupt and the maximum duration of each interrupt handler, and getting those numbers right, conservatively but not so conservatively that the analysis fails, is delicate.

The third is that **the worst case dominates and the worst case is the hard thing to establish**. A pipeline that runs in four hundred microseconds on the inputs the developers tried, and in twelve hundred microseconds on an input they did not try, is non-conformant, and no amount of testing reveals the twelve-hundred-microsecond input if testing never generates it. The kernel's worst-case response time is established by a bounded model checker that explores the input space symbolically, and setting that tool up — modelling the pipeline in a form the checker can analyse, choosing the bounds within which the checker explores, interpreting the checker's results — is specialised work. It is the work that converts a measured "fast enough so far" into a proven "fast enough always", and it is the work that the project's evidence-level-L1 claims rest on.

## 10. The kernel's boundaries

A frank chapter on the kernel must say what the kernel is not and does not do.

The kernel is not a general-purpose real-time operating system. It runs one fixed task set, under one scheduling discipline, against one set of timing bounds. It does not offer dynamic task creation as a general facility, does not offer a choice of scheduling disciplines, does not offer the rich set of synchronisation primitives a general-purpose RTOS provides. An implementer who needs those things needs a different piece of software; the kernel is specialised to the BCI signal-processing role and is deliberately narrow.

The kernel does not, by itself, provide the privacy and consent guarantees; it participates in them. The capability system is enforced at the boundary the kernel emits across, but the closed set of capabilities and the structural prohibitions are properties of the whole architecture, defined in the Standard, not invented by the kernel. The consent state machine is consulted by the kernel before each publication, but the consent subsystem is its own component with its own specification. The kernel is the timing substrate; the privacy and consent properties are system properties that the kernel cooperates with.

The kernel does not defend against an attacker who can replace the kernel image. Its guarantees are guarantees about the behaviour of the kernel that is actually running; an adversary with the ability to flash a modified kernel can make the modified kernel do anything. Defending the kernel image's integrity is the job of a secure-boot mechanism, which verifies the kernel's signature before executing it, and on stimulation-driving deployments of the Cognitive Hypervisor's Secure-World partitioning. Those are real defences and they exist, but they are not the kernel's, and the kernel does not claim them.

## 11. The kernel and the other subsystems

The kernel does not stand alone. It is the centre of a system, and it cooperates with the other components at well-defined interfaces.

To the **acquisition layer** below it, the kernel presents the acquisition interface: a direct-memory-access channel by which samples arrive, with an interrupt signalling completion. The kernel's timing analysis treats that interrupt as a bounded source of interference.

To the **software development kit** above it, the kernel presents the application binary interface: the handshake, the twenty-eight-byte wire format, the error taxonomy. The kernel emits intent observations across this interface and the software development kit consumes them.

With the **consent subsystem**, the kernel interlocks: before publishing any intent observation, the kernel consults the consent state, and a suspended or withdrawn state suppresses the publication and substitutes the appropriate error code. The consent state machine is its own specified component, but its enforcement point — the place where a consent state actually stops data — is inside the kernel's publication path.

With the **Cognitive Hypervisor**, on stimulation-driving deployments, the kernel cooperates across the Secure-World boundary: the Hypervisor reads the consent state, through a Secure-Monitor call, before permitting any stimulation pulse, so that a withdrawal of consent disables stimulation as well as recording.

## 12. The kernel's measured behaviour

The reference kernel, on the reference hardware, has been characterised by a soak test of at least twelve hours' duration, comprising on the order of ten million eight hundred thousand processing epochs, and the characterisation is the evidence behind the dual-core contract's measured clauses. Over that soak the worst observed end-to-end response time was nine hundred and seventy-two microseconds, against the one-millisecond proven bound, and the number of missed deadlines was zero. The observation-cadence jitter was two point one microseconds at one standard deviation. The inter-process-communication slot latency was two-tenths of a microsecond. These figures, with their evidence artefacts, are catalogued authoritatively in the validation material of the Standard; they are reproduced here to make concrete the claim that the kernel, as built, satisfies the contract it is designed to satisfy, and satisfies it with margin — and the margin is itself a design output, because a kernel that met its bound with no margin would be a kernel one revision away from violating it.

## Appendix to this chapter — A deeper look at three decisions

The body of this chapter described the kernel at the level an architect needs. This appendix goes one level deeper into three specific decisions, for the implementer who must actually write the corresponding code, because each of the three is a place where a plausible-seeming alternative is wrong in a way that is not obvious until it is explained.

### A.1 Why the publication task has the nearest deadline of all tasks

The inter-process-communication publication task is, in worst-case execution time, by far the smallest task in the set: two-tenths of a microsecond, against the eighty to three hundred and twenty microseconds of the signal-processing stages. A naive scheduling intuition would place such a tiny task wherever is convenient, on the reasoning that two-tenths of a microsecond cannot matter. The intuition is wrong, and the reason it is wrong illuminates how Earliest-Deadline-First actually behaves.

The publication task does not merely have to run; it has to run *last in the epoch*, after the classifier has produced the result it publishes, and it has to run *before the epoch ends*, because the result it publishes is this epoch's result and a result published in the next epoch is a result delivered late. Its relative deadline is therefore the end of the epoch, and because the end of the epoch is, for the publication task, very close — the publication task becomes ready only when the classifier finishes, late in the epoch — its absolute deadline at the moment it becomes ready is the nearest absolute deadline in the system. Earliest-Deadline-First, seeing the publication task become ready with the nearest deadline, runs it immediately, preempting nothing because nothing else is ready at that point, and the publication completes in its two-tenths of a microsecond with the epoch's remaining budget to spare.

The lesson for the implementer is that the publication task's tiny execution time and its late, tight deadline are two sides of one design: the task is tiny *so that* it can be run last, in the sliver of the epoch that remains after the classifier, and still complete before the epoch boundary. If the publication task were large, it could not fit in that sliver, and the pipeline would have to be restructured to give it room. The kernel keeps the publication task tiny — it does nothing but the fixed write sequence of Section 7.2 — precisely so that the scheduling works out.

### A.2 Why the artefact-rejection stage runs where it does

The artefact-rejection stage is the fourth of the six signal-processing stages, and a reader might reasonably ask why it is fourth. It flags epochs corrupted by muscle movement or electrode disturbance; would it not be better to run it first, and skip the expensive later stages entirely on a corrupted epoch, saving the time?

The answer is that the worst-case analysis does not benefit from skipping. The worst-case response time is the time of the *worst* epoch, and the worst epoch is precisely the one that is *not* skipped — the clean epoch that runs the full pipeline. Running artefact rejection first, and skipping the later stages on corrupted epochs, would reduce the *average* time, because some epochs are corrupted and would skip; but it would not reduce the *worst-case* time, because the worst case is a clean epoch, and a clean epoch runs everything regardless of where artefact rejection sits. And the worst-case time is what the Standard bounds and what the user feels.

Given that early artefact rejection does not improve the worst case, the stage is placed where it is most *useful*, which is after the filtering. Artefact rejection examines the signal for the characteristic signatures of corruption, and those signatures are clearer in the filtered signal than in the raw one — the filtering has already removed the mains interference and the out-of-band noise that would otherwise confuse the artefact detector. So the stage runs fourth, after the three filtering stages, because that is where it does its job best, and the worst-case argument is indifferent to the placement so the placement is free to be chosen on the merits.

This is a recurring pattern in the kernel's design, and the implementer should internalise it: optimisations that improve the average case but not the worst case are, for this kernel, not improvements at all, and the design is free to ignore them and to make the corresponding decisions on other grounds. The worst case is the master, and the average case is not consulted.

### A.3 Why the kernel forbids `unsafe` even though the hardware layer needs it

Section 3.1 stated that the kernel forbids `unsafe` entirely and reaches hardware through a separate hardware-abstraction layer that is permitted `unsafe`. An implementer might ask why the boundary is drawn there — why not permit `unsafe` in the kernel too, in just the few places that touch hardware, and dispense with the separate layer?

The answer is about the size and reviewability of the trusted base. Every line of `unsafe` code is a line where the compiler's memory-safety guarantee is suspended and a human's reasoning is substituted for it. The total quantity of `unsafe` in a system is the size of the part of the system whose memory safety rests on human reasoning rather than compiler enforcement, and the discipline of safety-critical engineering is to make that part as small, as isolated, and as intensely reviewed as possible.

If `unsafe` were permitted throughout the kernel, then the part of the system resting on human reasoning would be scattered across the whole kernel, a few lines here and a few lines there, intermixed with the thousands of lines of ordinary kernel logic. Auditing it would mean auditing the whole kernel for the `unsafe` occurrences and then reasoning about each in its local context. By confining `unsafe` to a separate hardware-abstraction layer, the kernel makes the part resting on human reasoning a single, small, separate module — a few hundred lines, all in one place, all doing the one kind of thing (touching hardware registers) that genuinely requires `unsafe`. That module can be audited as a unit, intensely, by a reviewer who reads all of it and understands all of it; and the kernel proper, the thousands of lines of scheduler and pipeline and channel logic, is then known to be memory-safe by compiler enforcement, with no audit of *its* memory safety required at all because the compiler has already done that audit.

The boundary is drawn where it is because drawing it there minimises and isolates the trusted base. The separate hardware-abstraction layer is not bureaucratic overhead; it is the mechanism by which the kernel's memory-safety story becomes "a few hundred audited lines plus a compiler guarantee" instead of "a whole kernel that must be audited for scattered `unsafe`."

## B. The kernel's development history, briefly

*Informative, and offered because the history explains the design.*

The kernel was not designed correct on the first attempt; no kernel is. Its history has three phases worth recording, because each phase left a mark on the present design.

The first phase was the C prototype, described in Section 3.1, which was abandoned after three months because C's safety model did not scale to the project's needs. The mark this phase left is the present kernel's absolute commitment to compiler-enforced memory safety: the project chose Rust and `forbid(unsafe_code)` not from language fashion but from direct, costly experience of the alternative.

The second phase was the first Rust kernel, which was memory-safe from the start but which initially missed its timing bound: the unoptimised pipeline's worst-case response time exceeded the one-millisecond target, at around one point eight milliseconds. The mark this phase left is the present kernel's tightly optimised pipeline stages and its honest accounting of how much work the optimisation was: the nine-hundred-and-seventy-two-microsecond figure was earned, stage by stage, and the chapter says so rather than presenting the figure as though it had been easy.

The third phase, the present one, is the kernel that meets the contract with margin and whose timing is established by proof rather than by measurement alone. The mark this phase left is the present kernel's investment in the bounded-model-checking infrastructure: the project came to understand, through the second phase, that a measured "fast enough" was not the same as a proven "fast enough always", and built the verification harness that converts the one into the other.

An implementer building an independent kernel should expect a similar arc. The memory-safety discipline can be adopted from the start, because the project's experience is available to learn from. But the timing will be tight, the first pipeline will likely miss the bound, the optimisation will be real work, and the move from measurement to proof will be a distinct and specialised effort. The chapter records this so that the implementer is not surprised, and does not mistake the difficulty for a sign of having done something wrong.

## C. Frequently raised objections, and the answers

*Informative. Over the course of presenting the kernel's design to reviewers, embedded-systems engineers, and prospective implementers, a recurring set of objections has emerged. They are good objections — they are the questions a competent engineer asks — and answering them in writing serves the reader better than leaving them implicit.*

**"A 168-megahertz microcontroller is slow. Why not a faster core, or an application-specific integrated circuit, and buy yourself enormous timing margin?"**

The objection assumes that more margin is straightforwardly better, and within limits it is, but the objection undervalues two things the commodity Cortex-M4F provides that a faster or custom part would not. The first is determinism of instruction timing: the Cortex-M4F's simple in-order pipeline means each instruction's cycle count is documented and fixed, and the worst-case-execution-time analysis can be tight. A faster application-processor-class core, of the kind that would offer more raw speed, achieves that speed partly through speculative execution and deep cache hierarchies whose timing is data-dependent in ways that make a tight worst-case bound much harder to establish; the faster core would have a better average case and a worse-characterised worst case, which is the wrong trade for this Standard. The second is reproducibility: the commodity part can be bought by anyone, so the project's evidence-level-L2 measurements can be reproduced by anyone, and a custom application-specific integrated circuit, however fast, would make the project's central measurements unreproducible by any party without access to the project's own silicon. The 168-megahertz commodity part is not a compromise the project regrets; it is a deliberate choice of a part whose timing is analysable and whose measurements are reproducible, and those properties are worth more to the Standard than raw speed.

**"The 0.25 utilisation ceiling wastes three-quarters of the processor. That is extravagant."**

It would be extravagant if the processor's cycles were the scarce resource, but they are not. The reference task set needs about 0.16 of the processor, and the epoch is 250 hertz; the kernel is not short of cycles, and using more of them would buy nothing the kernel needs. What the kernel is short of, and what it spends the apparent extravagance to buy, is *confidence in the worst-case bound*. The gap between the 0.25 ceiling and the 1.0 theoretical bound absorbs the interrupt load, the imprecision in the worst-case-execution-time estimates, and the micro-architectural effects the periodic-task model abstracts away. Calling that gap "waste" assumes the cycles in it could be usefully spent, but they cannot: spending them would mean admitting a task set whose timing the analysis could no longer conservatively guarantee, and a guarantee the analysis cannot conservatively make is not a guarantee. The "waste" is the price of the guarantee, the guarantee is the product, and the price is one the kernel pays gladly because it has cycles to spare and confidence to buy.

**"Forbidding `unsafe` entirely is dogmatic. Skilled engineers write correct `unsafe` Rust every day."**

They do, and the objection is fair as far as it goes. Correct `unsafe` Rust is entirely possible and a great deal of excellent software, including the Rust standard library, depends on it. The kernel's position is not that `unsafe` is impossible to use correctly; it is that *the size of the code that must be audited for memory safety* is a quantity worth minimising, and that `forbid(unsafe_code)` on the kernel proper minimises it. The kernel does not forbid `unsafe` in the system; the hardware-abstraction layer uses it. The kernel forbids `unsafe` in *itself*, so that the kernel's thousands of lines need no memory-safety audit at all and the audit effort concentrates on the hardware layer's few hundred. This is not dogma; it is the allocation of a scarce resource — expert audit attention — to where it does the most good, and the policy that makes that allocation possible is the blanket `forbid` on the large component so that the audit can focus on the small one.

**"The kernel does almost nothing. Six filter stages and a classifier — that is a few thousand lines. Where is the difficulty?"**

The difficulty is not in the quantity of code; the objection is right that the kernel is not large. The difficulty is in the *proof obligation* attached to the code. A few thousand lines of ordinary application software carry no obligation beyond working on the inputs tried. The kernel's few thousand lines carry the obligation of a proven worst-case response time over the entire input space, a proven set of finite-state-machine properties for the consent integration, a proven bound on the inter-process-communication latency, and a memory-safety guarantee — and the proofs, not the lines, are the work. A line of code that is easy to write can be attached to a proof obligation that is hard to discharge, and the kernel is exactly that situation: modest code, demanding proofs. The difficulty of Section 9 is the difficulty of the proofs, and the proofs do not shrink just because the code is small.

**"Why a separate kernel at all? A real-time Linux, with the `PREEMPT_RT` patch, can achieve low latency on a single capable processor."**

A real-time-patched Linux can indeed achieve impressively low latency, and for many soft-real-time applications it is the right tool. It is not the right tool here for two reasons. The first is the worst case: a real-time-patched Linux improves the latency distribution dramatically but remains a large, general-purpose system whose worst-case latency, over the whole space of inputs and system states, is extremely hard to bound by proof — there is simply too much code, too much state, too much that can happen. The Standard requires an evidence-level-L1 proof of the worst-case response time, and that proof is tractable for the AxonOS kernel's few thousand analysable lines and intractable for a general-purpose operating system's millions. The second reason is the privacy architecture: the capability system's structural prohibitions are properties of a small, purpose-built interface, and retrofitting them onto a general-purpose operating system's far larger and more permissive interface would be a vastly harder task than building the small interface correctly from the start. The separate kernel exists because the proof and the privacy architecture both require a small, purpose-built, analysable system, and a general-purpose operating system, real-time-patched or not, is not that.

## D. A note to the independent implementer

This chapter, and the Standard it accompanies, exist so that an independent implementer — a team with no connection to the AxonOS Project — can build a conformant kernel. This closing note is addressed directly to that implementer.

You do not need the reference kernel's source code to build a conformant kernel. You need the Standard, which defines the contracts; you need the conformance suite, which is the executable test of whether you have honoured them; and you need this chapter and its siblings, which explain the reasoning so that your hundred small decisions converge with the reference kernel's hundred small decisions rather than diverging from them. Your kernel may be written in a different language, may run on a different processor, may schedule its tasks with a different implementation of the Earliest-Deadline-First discipline, and may share not a line with the reference kernel. If it honours the contracts and passes the suite, it is conformant, exactly as conformant as the reference, and the Standard is designed to make that outcome reachable.

Three pieces of advice, distilled from the project's own experience. First: adopt the memory-safety discipline from the start, whatever your language; the cost of retrofitting safety onto an unsafe foundation is, the project found, far higher than the cost of building safe from the beginning. Second: do not believe a measured worst case; the worst case you measure is the worst of the inputs you tried, and conformance requires the worst over inputs you did not try, which only a proof delivers — budget for the proof work as a distinct and specialised effort, not as an afterthought to testing. Third: expect the timing to be tight and the optimisation to be real; the reference pipeline missed its bound on the first attempt, and yours likely will too, and that is not a sign of error but the normal shape of the work.

The Standard is the contract. The conformance suite is the test. This chapter is the reasoning. With those three, the independent kernel is reachable, and the project intends that it be reached — a Standard with only one implementation is a Standard that has not yet proven it is a Standard at all.
